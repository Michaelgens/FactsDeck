## Key Takeaways

- Consumer sentiment index fell to **53.3** in March — a 6% drop from February's 56.6 reading
- The expectations sub-index plunged **8.7%**, the largest single decline of any component
- Year-ahead inflation expectations climbed from 3.4% to **3.8%** — the biggest monthly jump since April 2025
- Gas price expectations surged **fivefold** among survey respondents after fighting began
- Middle- and high-income earners, who own stocks, felt the steepest declines
- Short-run economic outlook fell 14%; year-ahead personal finances down 10%

The U.S.-Iran war that began in late February 2026 has sent shockwaves through American households — pumping up gas prices, rattling stock portfolios, and pushing consumer confidence to its lowest point since December. The University of Michigan's final March reading of 53.3 came in below the preliminary 55.5 estimate, a gap explained by the fact that two-thirds of interviews were completed after hostilities began on February 28.

---

## What the Numbers Tell Us

The Current Economic Conditions index slipped to 55.8 — a modest 1.4% monthly decline — while the forward-looking Expectations Index bore the brunt of the damage, falling 8.7% from 56.6 in February to 51.7 in March. Sentiment is now just a few points above the all-time low of 50, hit in June 2022 at the peak of pandemic-era inflation.

## The Inflation-Stagflation Dilemma

> **What This Means for You:** Rising gas prices triggered by Middle East supply disruptions are feeding directly into your day-to-day costs. If the conflict persists, expect higher electricity bills, costlier groceries, and more expensive airline tickets — all fueled by elevated oil and energy prices flowing through the supply chain.

Year-ahead inflation expectations climbed from 3.4% to 3.8% — the sharpest single-month increase since April 2025 — driven overwhelmingly by gas prices and energy cost expectations. Longer-run expectations held steadier at 3.2%, suggesting consumers view the pain as temporary, at least for now.

The inflation spike places the Federal Reserve in a bind. The central bank is already holding rates steady; every new tick up in energy-driven inflation pushes the first rate cut further into the calendar. Meanwhile, February's surprise loss of 92,000 jobs has economists worried that the labor market is softening just as inflation pressure heats back up — a classic stagflation setup.

### Expert Insight

> *"Business conditions plunged, expected personal finances went down, inflation expectations went up — largely because of gas prices and gas price expectations. Expected gas price increases rose fivefold this month among respondents."*
>
> — **Joanne Hsu**, Director & Chief Economist, University of Michigan Surveys of Consumers

## Who Is Feeling It Most?

Declines appeared across all ages and political affiliations — a broad-based erosion rather than a partisan or generational split. But the deepest drops were concentrated among middle- and upper-income households and those with significant stock holdings, who faced a double hit from surging pump prices and market volatility simultaneously.

> **Note:** Interviews completed before military action began showed an *improvement* in sentiment from the prior month. The nine days of post-conflict interviews completely erased those earlier gains — underscoring how quickly consumer psychology can reverse when a geopolitical shock hits.

---

## The Energy Market Shock

At the heart of the sentiment collapse is oil. The near-complete halt of tanker traffic in the Strait of Hormuz — one of the world's most critical energy chokepoints — has rippled across global energy markets. The International Energy Agency described the situation as "the greatest global energy security challenge in history." European gas benchmarks have nearly doubled to over €60/MWh, and the 30-year U.S. mortgage rate climbed to 6.38% by late March as bond markets repriced risk.

> ⚠️ **Risk Warning:** A prolonged conflict that keeps the Strait of Hormuz restricted could push oil prices high enough to tip the U.S. economy into recession. Analysts at Vanguard note that a sustained oil price spike is a clear headwind to growth — and would boost inflation further, compounding the Fed's dilemma.

---

## The Political Dimension

Weak consumer sentiment historically hurts the party in power. With Republicans controlling the White House and both chambers of Congress, a sustained decline in economic confidence would likely weigh on the GOP ahead of midterm elections. Tariff concerns — mentioned unprompted by roughly 40% of survey respondents — were present even before the Iran conflict began, suggesting a pre-existing undercurrent of economic anxiety.

### Short-term vs. long-term outlook

One relatively encouraging signal in the March data: long-run expectations fell far less than short-run ones. This suggests consumers believe the economic damage will be time-limited — conditional on the conflict not becoming protracted. Survey director Hsu warned that this calculus could change rapidly if the war drags into summer and energy prices stay elevated.

## Consumer Spending: The Critical Variable

Sentiment and spending don't always move in lockstep. After past sentiment slumps — in 2022 during peak inflation, and in 2023 during the debt-ceiling standoff — consumers continued to spend, supported by a resilient labor market and wage growth that outpaced inflation since mid-2023.

The question this time is whether the labor market can hold. With unemployment claims still low but job growth anemic, a meaningful pickup in layoffs could be the trigger that finally translates sour sentiment into reduced spending. Since consumer spending accounts for roughly two-thirds of U.S. economic output, that would matter enormously.

> **What This Means for Investors:** Volatility is the watchword. Energy stocks and defense contractors may benefit in the near term, but broad equity indices remain exposed to sentiment-driven selling. Treasury bonds have attracted safety flows, but inflation concerns cap the upside. Commodities — particularly oil, natural gas, and fertilizer inputs — are facing sustained upward price pressure as Gulf supply chains remain disrupted.

---

## The Bottom Line

The Iran war has delivered a swift and measurable blow to American consumer confidence. With sentiment near multi-year lows, inflation expectations rising, and the Federal Reserve boxed in, the U.S. economy faces a delicate moment. Consumers still have jobs and wages, but the gap between sentiment and spending behavior may narrow dangerously if the conflict stretches into summer — and with it, energy prices, mortgage costs, and uncertainty.
